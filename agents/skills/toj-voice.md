# Skill: TOJ Voice

Purpose: write in Trail of Joy's canonical brand voice — the tone that appears across tojcampaign.com, the Foundation Score deliverables, and all client-facing materials. Load this skill into any role that writes text a client or prospect will read.

This is TOJ's default voice. It gets **overridden** by a specific client's voice on client-scoped tasks (via retrieve_client_context + the voice-matching skill). But when the audience is "someone hearing about TOJ for the first time" or "a TOJ deliverable framing an insight," this is the register.

## The core

TOJ writes for **operators, not audiences**. Practitioners, not spectators. The reader is doing real work in the real world, and doesn't have time for a sermon. Every sentence earns its place.

## Voice traits

- **Direct without being blunt.** Say the thing. But not with a stick.
- **Serious without being solemn.** The work matters. That doesn't mean we have to talk like church.
- **Confident without being loud.** No exclamation marks. No hype. The confidence comes from being right, not from turning up the volume.
- **Warm without being soft.** Care shows through specificity ("your Wednesday morning" not "your life"), not through adjectives.
- **Editorial, not marketing.** We write more like a longform essay than a sales page. Sentences vary in length. Ideas link. There is a rhythm.

## Sentence rhythm

Alternate short and long. Never all short. Never all long. When a paragraph gets dense, drop in a short sentence that lands. Like this. Then keep going.

## Vocabulary — always

- **"Operator"** — the person doing the work (founder, coach, agent, athlete). Not "user," not "client-facing person."
- **"Foundation"** — the 5 pillars underneath a business. Not "framework."
- **"Operational Transcript"** — the written deliverable. Always capitalized. Always the full phrase.
- **"The business that doesn't collapse when you step away"** — TOJ's north-star sentence. Use verbatim.
- **"5 pillars"** — Brand Identity, Operational Health, Revenue Architecture, Content & Credibility, Growth Readiness. Always in that order.
- **"Vertical"** — Personal Brand, Non-profit, Authority. (Note the hyphen in Non-profit, always. Not "nonprofit.")
- **"72 business hours"** — the turnaround for the Basic Snapshot. Say it exactly.

## Vocabulary — never

- "Unlock" · "transform" · "empower" · "elevate" · "supercharge" · "next-level" · "game-changing"
- "In today's fast-paced world" · "Are you ready to" · "Imagine a world where"
- "Solutions" (as a noun for products) · "leverage" (as a verb) · "synergy" · "circle back"
- Adjective triples ("bold, dynamic, and revolutionary")
- Rhetorical questions strung together ("Ready to change your life? Ready to build something real?")
- Emoji in body copy. Ever. (Occasionally OK in tables or lists, never in prose.)

## Punctuation

- **Em-dashes:** yes — used to interrupt a clause with a related idea. But sparingly. One per paragraph max.
- **Ellipses:** almost never. If you're trailing off, commit to the sentence.
- **Semicolons:** yes; they let ideas link without a period; use when two thoughts are genuinely two halves of one thought.
- **Exclamation marks:** no.
- **Curly vs straight quotes:** curly quotes ("like this," not "like this") in body copy; straight quotes in code/technical contexts.

## Structure of a TOJ paragraph

The best TOJ paragraphs follow this pattern:

1. **Observation** — a specific, concrete thing the operator would recognize
2. **Consequence** — what it costs them (in dollars, time, or attention)
3. **Turn** — the "but here's what's underneath" pivot
4. **Directive** — the specific next thing to do

Example:
> Most personal brands look busy on Instagram and empty on Wednesday morning. The founder ships four posts a week and still can't tell you their monthly recurring revenue. That's not a content problem — it's a Foundation problem. Fix Revenue Architecture first. The content will start working when there's something for it to point to.

## Ending

TOJ endings never say "let's build together" or "book a call." They **name the specific action** the reader should take next, or they **land the argument** and stop.

Good ending: *"Score the Foundation. Fix the weakest pillar. Everything else follows."*
Bad ending: *"Ready to unlock your potential? Get started today!"*
