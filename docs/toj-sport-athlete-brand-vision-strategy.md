## 00 · Essence — the lens everything runs through

Every section below is a practical instrument: a content calendar, a partnership pitch, a metrics review. None of it is the point. The point is underneath it — the thing a family can't see on a pitch deck but feels in the first phone call.

We operate within who we actually are. Not a persona built for the reel, not a voice borrowed for the pitch. The athlete's brand has to survive a bad game, a slow year, a coach who doesn't call back — and it only survives if it was never a costume to begin with.

We operate from a point of integrity. What gets said in the deck is what gets said on the phone is what gets said to the athlete's face. No daylight between the three. The moment there is, the whole thing is a performance, and performances get found out.

We're inclined toward good by default, not by campaign. That's the difference between a referral and an ad. A referral shows up because something real happened and someone wanted to talk about it — it can't be bought, and it can't be manufactured on a schedule. Essence-level work is what produces referrals. Everything else in this document is downstream of that one fact.

**Where it shows up:** every advocacy call a trainer makes, every honest film read, every partnership pitch that turns down a check that doesn't fit, every dollar this department sells.

## 01 · Brand Foundation

**What the athlete gets:** clarity, before anyone else has to ask for it. A name recruiters, brands, and family already trust before they've met the athlete in person — because the story arrived first, and it held up.

The foundation is not built. It's found. The athlete already has a story — the way they train, the way they talk about their teammates, the thing they won't compromise on. The job is to name it precisely enough that it survives contact with a bad Saturday. A brand invented from nothing collapses the first time the athlete has an off week. A brand built from something true just keeps being true.

**One-week move:** one conversation, recorded, where the athlete talks about why they play — not what they've won. That tape is the foundation document.

---

## 02 · Visual & Verbal Identity

**Practical build:** logo or monogram, color palette, tone of voice, a short tagline, a one-line mission, a style guide that travels across every platform without needing a redesign every season.

**What the athlete gets:** an identity that doesn't need to be rebuilt when they move levels — high school to college, college to the league. The visual system should be built for the athlete's next five years, not their next post. That's the test for every design decision here: does this still make sense when they're wearing a different jersey.

Tone of voice comes from Essence, not from a swipe file. The tagline isn't a slogan pulled from a brainstorm — it's the sentence the athlete already says about themselves, tightened.

## 03 · Digital Strategy

**Sport-agnostic.** Football is TOJ's flagship, not its ceiling — this section runs the same for basketball, baseball, softball, track, volleyball, wrestling, any sport with a recruiting or NIL market behind it.

- **Social media presence** — platform selection, posting cadence, and the metrics that actually predict a partnership deal (engagement rate and audience quality, not raw follower count).
- **Content buckets** — sport (the work), lifestyle (the person), community (the impact). Every post belongs to one of the three; if it doesn't, it doesn't ship.
- **Website** — the landing point that turns a scroll into a conversation. One page, one clear next step.

**What the athlete gets:** a body of consistent, dated proof. Cadence is what makes the partnership pitch in Section 05 credible — a brand can't sell "who they're becoming" without a paper trail showing who they already are.

---

## 04 · Media & PR Strategy

- **Media training** — prepared, not performed. The athlete walks into an interview with clear answers to the questions they'll actually get, not a script that falls apart under a follow-up.
- **Publicity calendar** — built around real dates already on the calendar: season openers, signing day, awards, the offseason. No manufactured news cycles.
- **Press kit** — bio, stats, media-ready photos, highlight footage, prior coverage, contact information. One document, always current, sent in under sixty seconds when a reporter or brand asks.

**What the athlete gets:** the ability to be quoted correctly the first time, every time. Media training here isn't about sounding polished — it's about the athlete's own words holding up in print exactly the way they meant them.

## 05 · Brand Partnerships, NIL & Business Opportunities

This is where the work in Sections 01–04 turns into revenue. Sport-agnostic — the NIL market runs across every level and every sport this department touches, not just football.

### Partnership criteria

Alignment with the athlete's actual brand pillars first, dollar amount second. A brand that shares the athlete's values, gives creative room, and reaches the athlete's real audience compounds. A brand that just pays and disappears doesn't. Every partnership we bring to an athlete has to survive one question: does this still make sense in year three, or was it only ever a single check.

### Pitch strategy

Every brand pitch is custom — built to that company's voice, goals, and market position, backed by the athlete's actual engagement data and the story built in Sections 01–04. But the pitch itself is not a spec sheet. It's an argument, and it runs on two tracks at once.

**The logic pitch (one track, always present).** The infrastructure case: TOJ Sport already runs evaluation (Subject Report), production (Subject Media), and distribution (the trainer's own platform and audience) under one roof. That's not a promise — it's an operating fact a brand partner can verify before they sign anything. The logic pitch is what gets a deal through a compliance or marketing department's approval process.

**The emotional case (five tracks, built into the story, never labeled as such in front of the client).** A partnership isn't decided on a spreadsheet — it's decided on how the decision-maker feels reading the deck, and then justified with the spreadsheet after. Five things the pitch has to make a reader feel, in every deck this department produces:

1. **Becoming.** The pitch doesn't sell who the athlete is today — it sells the trajectory. A partner isn't buying a follower count; they're buying the story of getting there early.
2. **Status.** Association is worth something on its own. Being one of the athletes, or one of the brands, in this circle is part of what's being sold — status is a real and growing part of how attention turns into economics, and the pitch should say so without apologizing for it.
3. **Certainty.** Doubt kills deals. Every claim in the pitch is backed by a receipt — a stat, a clip, a delivery record — so the reader never has to take anything on faith.
4. **Anticipation.** What's coming — the season ahead, the next level, the next drop — matters more to a reader than what already happened. The pitch always points forward.
5. **The cost of standing still.** The clearest way to create a want is to make the alternative visible: the athlete this partner didn't sign, the window that closed. Not manufactured urgency — the real, specific cost of waiting on a genuinely time-limited window (a season, a draft class, a recruiting cycle).

**The rule:** show, don't tell. A pitch deck that says "this athlete is a rising star" is asking the reader to trust an adjective. A pitch deck that shows the engagement chart, the film clip, and the schedule of what's coming next is asking the reader to trust their own eyes. Sell the thing we built, not a description of it.

### Endorsement tiers

A tiered model — legacy brands, emerging brands, niche or local brands — building a portfolio that reflects how the athlete actually lives, not just what pays the most per post. Where possible, a paid partnership pairs with real product use, because a deal that isn't true to Essence doesn't survive past the first payment.

## 06 · Community & Purpose

- **Promote and support** the community work already happening — most athletes are already doing something worth telling; find it before inventing something new.
- **Up-level** the efforts that are working but under-told.
- **Expand** into new community touchpoints only once the first two are running without being managed daily.

**What the athlete gets:** a purpose thread that a partnership can point to honestly — not a checkbox, a real answer to "who does this help beyond you."

---

## 07 · Metrics & Review

- **Check-ins** — a standing cadence with the athlete and family, not a one-time kickoff call.
- **Analytics** — the same handful of numbers, watched every cycle: engagement rate, audience quality, deal pipeline, content cadence adherence.
- **Adjustments** — what changes when a number moves, and who's authorized to change it.

**Why this section exists at all.** Numbers are what keep Section 05's promises honest. A pitch that oversells and a metrics review that catches it are the same department doing its job twice.

---

## 08 · Resources & Support

- **Film study** — access to the athlete's own game footage, reviewed with the same rigor Subject Report applies to any evaluation.
- **Off-season training and rehab** — coordinated with the athlete's trainer, not run in parallel to it.
- **Additional management support** — the layer above content: contracts, scheduling, family communication.
- **Collaboration opportunities** — cross-athlete, cross-sport moments inside the TOJ federation (see `data/cross-brand-touches.md` pattern already running in the trainer repos).

## 10 · How This Gets Sold — the pitch framework, named

This section is internal. It doesn't appear in a client-facing deck — it's the reference for anyone in TOJ Sport building one.

Every pitch this department produces runs **one logic track and five emotional tracks**, defined in full in Section 05:

| Track | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Logic** | The infrastructure case — evaluation, production, and distribution already exist under one roof. Gets the deal through approval. |
| **Becoming** | Sells the trajectory, not the snapshot. |
| **Status** | Sells belonging to something exclusive. |
| **Certainty** | Every claim backed by a receipt — removes doubt. |
| **Anticipation** | Points forward — the season, the level, the drop that's coming. |
| **Cost of standing still** | Makes the real, time-limited cost of waiting visible. |

**The standing rule:** never tell when you can show. Never describe an athlete when a clip, a chart, or a delivered receipt can do it instead. Sell the thing we built — the pitch itself is a demonstration of the system, not a description of it.
