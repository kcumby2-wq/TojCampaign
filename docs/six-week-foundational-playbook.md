# Week 1 — Brand Identity Fundamentals

## The deep dive

Positioning is the compressed answer to three questions:
1. **Who is this for** (and equally: who is this *not* for)?
2. **What is the alternative** the buyer is choosing between?
3. **Why you specifically** — the one true, defensible claim only you make.

The Futur (Chris Do) teaches this as "brand is what people say about you
when you're not in the room." The mistake first-time brand-builders make
is treating brand as logo, color, and voice. Those are downstream. Brand
starts with a *choice* — who you serve, what you're against, and what
you claim only you can do.

Marty Neumeier's **Zag** compresses the same idea: differentiation is
existential. If everyone in your category says X, you say not-X. A
"positioning statement" is just the sentence-form output of a real
positioning choice — but the choice comes first.

**Tone of voice** is downstream of positioning. It's three dials:
- **Register** — formal ↔ casual
- **Warmth** — clinical ↔ personal
- **Assertion** — hedging ↔ direct

Every brand picks a point on each dial and keeps it. Copy that drifts
across all three feels like the brand doesn't know itself yet.

**What to internalize from The Futur playlist:** the difference between
positioning (choice) and messaging (expression); the "for whom / against
whom / why us" framework; the discipline of saying no to buyers who
aren't the ICP.

# Week 2 — Service Design and SOPs

## The deep dive

A **service blueprint** is a stacked-lane map of what a customer
experiences vs. what your business does behind the curtain. NN/g's
canonical framework has five lanes:

1. **Physical evidence** — the artifacts the customer sees (site, PDF,
   email, deliverable)
2. **Customer actions** — what they do (fill intake, book call, sign)
3. **Frontstage actions** — what your team does that the customer sees
4. **Backstage actions** — what your team does that the customer doesn't
5. **Support processes** — the systems and third-parties (Stripe, GHL,
   Resend, calendar)

The blueprint's job is to expose the **moments of truth** — the specific
points where the customer's confidence is won or lost — and force you to
decide how each one is delivered. If a moment of truth has no owner,
no SLA, and no verification, that's a failure waiting to happen.

**SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures)** are the workflow-level detail
underneath each blueprint step. A good SOP has:

- **Purpose** — why this SOP exists in one sentence
- **Owner** — one named role, not a team
- **Trigger** — what event fires the SOP
- **Inputs** — what the owner needs to start
- **Steps** — numbered, one action per step, verb-first
- **QA / verification** — the check that proves the step happened right
- **Outputs** — what leaves the SOP for the next step
- **Failure modes** — the known ways this SOP breaks and the recovery

**What to internalize from NN/g:** the five-lane discipline; the
"moment of truth" concept; the reason blueprints beat process diagrams
(they show customer experience alongside internal work).

# Week 3 — Pricing and Revenue Architecture

## The deep dive

Blair Enns's *Win Without Pitching Manifesto* + *The Win Without Pitching
Guide* teaches four core moves:

1. **Position yourself as an expert practitioner, not a vendor.** Vendors
   respond to RFPs. Experts diagnose and prescribe.
2. **Never accept a project below your minimum.** The lower bound of
   your price is not the buyer's budget — it's the effort it takes for
   you to care.
3. **Value-price, don't cost-plus.** The client's outcome is what you
   sell. Your time is what you buy back to deliver it.
4. **Fixed fee, delivered by proposal.** Hourly billing anchors the
   buyer to time, not outcome. Fixed fee anchors both of you to result.

ProfitWell's core teachings on pricing pages:
- Anchor price high (make the expensive option visible first)
- Clear delta between tiers (each tier should have one dominant reason)
- Feature comparison must be scannable in 15 seconds
- Social proof must appear alongside price, not after

**Price fences** are the criteria that route a buyer to a tier —
without them, buyers self-select downward to whichever tier costs
least. Good fences are objective (team size, deployment complexity,
timeline urgency), not subjective (how sophisticated the buyer is).

**Discount policy** is the single most-abused pricing lever. The
Enns principle: *we discount access, not price.* If a buyer needs a
lower number, they give us something back — timing (delayed start),
scope (fewer deliverables), or exposure (case-study rights).

**What to internalize from Blair Enns:** the shift from vendor to
expert; the discipline of never quoting hourly; the confidence to
walk away from below-minimum work.

**What to internalize from ProfitWell:** the anchor-price effect;
the "one dominant reason per tier" principle; the social proof
adjacency rule.

# Week 4 — Content, Proof, and Distribution

## The deep dive

Ahrefs's content strategy teaches the **pillar-and-cluster** model:

- **Pillar page** — long, canonical article on a broad topic
  (e.g., "Operating systems for solo brands")
- **Cluster posts** — narrow, deep articles that each link back to the
  pillar (e.g., "Client intake automations for trainers," "Retention
  cadences for creators")
- **Topical authority** compounds when your site has depth on a
  narrow topic set, not breadth on a wide one

The right unit of ambition is the *cluster*, not the *post*. One pillar
+ eight to twelve cluster posts = a ranking asset.

CXL's case-study framework uses the McKinsey pyramid:

- **Situation** — where the buyer was
- **Complication** — what stopped them
- **Question** — the buyer's real question
- **Answer** — what you did, how, and the measured result

The best case studies name the buyer, name the metric, name the failure
mode you overcame, and show the artifact. Anonymous case studies convert
worse than no case studies — they signal fear.

**What to internalize from Ahrefs:** clusters compound; depth beats
breadth; every post should link to the pillar and to two clusters.

**What to internalize from CXL:** name the client; name the metric;
name the failure; show the artifact.

# Week 5 — Capacity, Delegation, Automation

## The deep dive

**RACI** (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) is the
canonical decision-rights framework:

- **R — Responsible:** the people doing the work
- **A — Accountable:** the one person who owns the outcome
- **C — Consulted:** advisors whose input is required before decision
- **I — Informed:** people who need to know the outcome after

Rule: exactly **one A** per activity. Zero A's means no one owns it.
Two A's means conflict. RACI's job is to expose ambiguity so it can
be resolved before work starts.

**Redline capacity rules** are the pre-set limits that stop a
one-person operation from over-committing. HBR on org design says:
capacity isn't the same as availability. Just because you have time on
the calendar doesn't mean you have cognitive headroom for another
active client. Redline rules encode this into the operation.

**Delegation** is the discipline of transferring outcome ownership, not
task ownership. HBR: the moment you delegate a task without delegating
the decision, you've created a bottleneck that runs through you. Real
delegation is scary — it means someone else can make the wrong call
with your name on it. Do it anyway.

**Automation** is the last mile — after you've clarified who owns what
(RACI), when you'll stop taking work (redlines), and what decisions the
owner can make without you (delegation). Zapier and its peers automate
*handoffs*, not decisions.

**What to internalize from HBR:** delegate outcomes, not tasks; capacity
≠ availability; one A per activity or you have conflict.

**What to internalize from Zapier tutorials:** automate handoffs
(intake → CRM → notification → follow-up), not judgment (does this
lead fit, should we take it, what should the proposal say).

# Week 6 — Measurement and Governance

## The deep dive

MeasureSchool's GA4 fundamentals teach the shift from *pageviews* to
*events*. GA4 measures *behavior* — every action the user takes is an
event, and conversions are the events you flag as valuable.

Core GA4 events for TOJ:
- `page_view` — automatic
- `foundation_score_intake_start` — user opens /foundation-score.html
- `foundation_score_intake_submit` — user submits the intake (conversion)
- `vertical_door_click` — user clicks a vertical door on home
- `letter_subscribe` — user submits the weekly letter form
- `ideas_modal_open` — user opens the "3 free ideas" modal
- `ideas_modal_submit` — user submits the "3 free ideas" form

PMI's discipline on retrospectives:
- Retrospectives run monthly minimum, quarterly max
- Format: *What went well / What went wrong / What we'll do differently*
- Every retrospective produces at least one **change decision** —
  otherwise it's therapy, not governance
- Change decisions get owners and due dates on the spot

**KPIs measure the pillars, not the activities.** The mistake first-time
founders make is measuring what's easy (visits, followers) instead of
what matters (conversion, retention, cycle time).
